i thought of an analogy: air brakes. i asked an LLM about why trucks use air brakes:
Heavy trucks, buses, and trains use **air brakes** instead of the hydraulic (fluid-based) brakes found in standard passenger cars primarily for reasons of **safety, reliability, and sheer stopping power**. 

When you are trying to stop a vehicle that can weigh up to 80,000 pounds (or more), the physics and risks change dramatically. Here is a breakdown of exactly why air brakes are the standard for heavy-duty vehicles:

### 1. The Ultimate "Fail-Safe" Design
This is the single most important reason trucks use air brakes. 

In a standard car, hydraulic brakes use fluid pressure to push the brake pads against the wheels. **If your car's brake line is cut and the fluid leaks out, you lose your ability to brake.** Air brakes in large trucks work on the exact opposite principle for their emergency and parking brakes, utilizing a **spring brake** system:
* **Default state is "Braking":** Inside the brake chamber at each wheel, there is a massive, heavy-duty mechanical spring that naturally wants to push the brakes *on*.
* **Air releases the brakes:** The truck's air compressor builds up air pressure. This pressure is used to push against that heavy spring, which pulls the brake pads *away* from the wheels, allowing the truck to roll. 
* **The Fail-Safe:** If an air line is cut, a valve breaks, or the compressor fails, the truck loses air pressure. Without air pressure holding the springs back, the springs automatically expand and slam the brakes on. 



Instead of a leak resulting in a runaway truck, a leak forces the truck to a sudden, safe stop.

### 2. An Infinite Supply of Air
Hydraulic systems rely on a finite reservoir of brake fluid. If you have a small leak in a hydraulic system, you will eventually run out of fluid and lose your brakes. 

Air systems, however, pull from the atmosphere. A truck's engine constantly runs an air compressor. If there is a minor leak in the system, the compressor simply pulls in more outside air and pumps it into the tanks to compensate until the truck can be serviced. 

### 3. Easy Trailer Coupling and Uncoupling
Semi-trucks constantly detach from one trailer and hook up to another. 
* **If trucks used hydraulic fluid:** Every time a driver disconnected a trailer, hydraulic fluid would leak everywhere. Every time they hooked up a new trailer, the brake lines would fill with air bubbles, and a mechanic would have to "bleed" the brake lines before the truck could be driven safely.
* **Because trucks use air:** Drivers can easily connect and disconnect the pressurized air lines (using connectors called "glad hands") without creating a mess or ruining the system's integrity. When the air lines are disconnected from the trailer, the trailer's fail-safe spring brakes automatically engage, locking the trailer safely in place so it doesn't roll away.

### 4. Raw Stopping Power
Hydraulic fluid is excellent for the braking force needed for a 4,000-pound car. However, to stop an 80,000-pound loaded semi-truck, the braking force required is immense. Compressed air systems can store and deliver a massive amount of consistent, highly regulated pressure across a long distance (from the front of the cab all the way to the back axles of a 53-foot trailer) much more efficiently than a hydraulic fluid system could at that scale.
